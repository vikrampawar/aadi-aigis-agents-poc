Upstream Oil & Gas Acquisition VDR Playbook
This document provides domain context for an AI assistant answering questions about upstream oil & gas M&A using a specific virtual data room (VDR). It covers valuation approaches, reserves evaluation and reporting, fiscal regimes, legal and regulatory due diligence, typical VDR structure, and practical heuristics and checklists.

1. Overview of Upstream Oil & Gas M&A
Upstream M&A focuses on acquiring or divesting interests in exploration and production (E&P) assets whose main value driver is hydrocarbon reserves and production. Buyers are concerned with the quality, quantity, and risk of reserves and resources, the cash flows they generate under different fiscal systems, and the legal and operational constraints attached to them. The VDR is the primary mechanism for sharing detailed technical, commercial, legal, and financial information during due diligence.

Key themes for buyers include:

Reserve and resource quality, uncertainty, and upside.

Cash‑flow robustness under different price decks, cost assumptions, and fiscal regimes.

Legal robustness of title, licenses, and contracts.

Environmental, decommissioning, and ESG risk.

Ability to realize synergies and operational improvements.

2. Valuation Methodologies in Upstream Oil & Gas
2.1 Standard valuation approaches
In upstream oil & gas, the standard valuation approaches (income, market, and asset/NAV) are all used, but their application is tailored to industry specifics.

Income / DCF approach:

Value is based on discounted future net cash flows from reserves and, sometimes, contingent resources.

Analysts model production profiles, commodity price decks, operating costs, capex, fiscal terms, and taxes to derive NPV metrics such as NPV10 and NPV15.

Sensitivity analysis on prices, costs, timing, and production is standard to assess downside and upside cases.

Market / comparables approach:

Uses trading and transaction multiples such as:

EV / proved reserves (e.g. EV/boe).

EV / daily production.

EV / EBITDAX.

Multiples are segmented by basin, onshore vs offshore, liquids vs gas, and asset risk profile.

Asset / NAV approach:

Builds a net asset value by valuing reserves and key assets individually and netting debt and other liabilities.

Common in upstream because the primary value is in reserves and associated infrastructure rather than corporate overhead.

2.2 Key modeling inputs and assumptions
Common modeling inputs the assistant should expect to see in the VDR include:

Price decks:

Base, high, and low scenarios, often derived from internal, consultant, or market curves.

Production profiles:

Historical production, decline curves, and forecast profiles for each field or well.

Operating costs:

Lifting costs, fixed and variable opex, transportation, and processing.

Capex:

Development drilling, facilities, workovers, and abandonment.

Fiscal terms and taxes:

Royalties, production sharing terms, bonuses, rentals, and income or special petroleum taxes.

Discount rates:

Often 10–15% for NPVs in upstream reserve evaluations, with higher rates for riskier assets.

Analysts typically run sensitivities on commodity prices, discount rates, capex and opex, production performance, and sometimes FX and government take to assess valuation robustness.

2.3 Practical valuation heuristics
Useful heuristics for interpreting valuation outputs include:

Higher risk assets (exploration, frontier basins, complex fiscal regimes, or heavy regulatory risk) justify higher discount rates and wider valuation ranges.

Assets with a high proportion of undeveloped reserves, complex PSC terms, or large decommissioning liabilities often trade at lower headline multiples than PDP‑heavy portfolios.

Multiples such as EV/boe of proved reserves or EV/production must be interpreted in the context of:

Reserve life, liquids/gas mix, cost structure, and fiscal regime.

Buyers often anchor on a base‑case NPV at a standard discount rate and then overlay strategic premiums or discounts for synergies, optionality, and perceived risk.

Government take and fiscal stability materially affect value; two assets with similar technical metrics can price differently if fiscal terms differ.

3. Petroleum Fiscal Regimes and Contracts
3.1 Main types of fiscal systems
Upstream petroleum projects are typically governed by one of three broad fiscal system archetypes, often combined in hybrid systems.

Tax and royalty (concession/licence) systems:

Investor (licensee) usually bears all costs and owns the produced hydrocarbons until fiscal payments are made.

Government take is primarily through royalties, corporate income tax, and sometimes additional rent‑capture mechanisms (e.g. resource rent taxes, special petroleum taxes, government equity).

Investor typically “books” all reserves, even though a portion of cash flow is paid as taxes and royalties.

Production Sharing Contracts (PSCs):

The “contractor” bears all exploration and development costs.

Gross production is allocated to:

Royalties or a minimum state share.

Cost‑recovery oil or gas (subject to caps such as a percentage of gross revenue).

Profit oil or gas shared between state and contractor, often on a progressive basis.

Contractor may pay income tax on their share of profit petroleum; variants can have government paying or “deeming” tax.

Contractor “books” only the reserves corresponding to their net entitlement share.

Service and risk‑service contracts:

State or national oil company retains title to hydrocarbons; the contractor is paid fees, sometimes linked to production or cost recovery.

In pure service contracts, the contractor may not book reserves, while in risk‑service contracts some reserve booking may be possible depending on accounting rules.

Government take is embedded in the fee structure and tax treatment rather than in explicit profit sharing.

Most countries implement hybrids, combining royalties, taxes, bonuses, and profit sharing, even in PSC or service contexts.

3.2 Key fiscal mechanisms and metrics
Common fiscal mechanisms affecting cash flows:

Upfront and recurring payments:

Signature, discovery, and production bonuses.

Rentals and license fees.

Royalties:

Fixed or sliding rates on gross production or revenue, often differentiated by resource type, price level, or production rate.

Cost recovery:

Caps on cost oil/gas as a percentage of production or revenue, with unrecovered costs carried forward.

Profit sharing:

Profit oil/gas split between state and contractor, sometimes based on R‑factor, production tranches, or internal rate of return.

Income and special petroleum taxes:

Corporate tax and sector‑specific surtaxes or resource rent taxes.

Government participation:

Carried or paying interests in licenses or PSCs.

Key metrics used by analysts:

Government take: share of project net cash flow captured by the state.

Contractor net entitlement: volumes and cash flow after all fiscal mechanisms, used for reserve booking and valuation.

Effective tax rate and breakevens under different price scenarios.

3.3 Fiscal regime heuristics for the assistant
When interpreting documents from different jurisdictions and contract types, the assistant should keep in mind:

Conceptual similarities:

Well‑designed PSC and tax‑and‑royalty systems can be economically equivalent; differences lie in mechanics and accounting.

Sensitivities:

High cost‑recovery caps and generous profit splits improve contractor economics but may be offset by higher taxes or bonuses.

Sliding royalties, windfall taxes, or progressive profit splits increase government take at higher prices.

Red flags:

Very high government take (e.g. at the top of 40–85% global ranges) combined with unstable terms can depress value and increase risk.

Ambiguous cost‑recoverability definitions, aggressive cost‑recovery claims, or disputes over cost audits.

Decommissioning and environmental obligations that are not clearly integrated into fiscal and tax assumptions.

4. Reserves and Resources: Classification, Evaluation, Reporting
4.1 Classification systems (PRMS, SEC, others)
Several frameworks govern how petroleum reserves and resources are classified and reported.

PRMS (Petroleum Resources Management System):

Distinguishes between discovered (reserves and contingent resources) and undiscovered (prospective resources).
​

Reserves are quantities anticipated to be commercially recoverable under defined conditions.
​

Reserves sub‑categories:

Proved, Probable, Possible.

Aggregate categories: 1P (Proved), 2P (Proved + Probable), 3P (Proved + Probable + Possible).

SEC reporting:

Focuses on proved reserves and applies “reasonable certainty” criteria.

Requires committed development plans for booking PUDs and conversion of PUDs to developed status within prescribed time frames.
​

Places restrictions on pricing assumptions and disclosure of unproved reserves.

Other frameworks (e.g. UNFC) exist but are less central in typical corporate M&A data rooms.

4.2 Reserve categories and typical metrics
Reserve categories the assistant should recognize:

PDP: Proved Developed Producing.

PDNP / PDN: Proved Developed Non‑Producing.

PUD: Proved Undeveloped.

Probable and Possible reserves, aggregated into 2P and 3P.

Typical reserve metrics in evaluations and reports:

Total reserves by category (1P, 2P, 3P) in boe, by field and basin.

Reserve life index (RLI) or R/P ratio (reserves divided by annual production).

Net vs gross reserves (after working interest, royalties, and other burdens).

Risked vs unrisked resource estimates for contingent and prospective resources.

4.3 Evaluation methods
Common reservoir and reserves evaluation methods include:

Volumetric methods:

Using rock and fluid properties, area, thickness, and saturation to estimate in‑place volumes.

Decline‑curve analysis:

Extrapolating historical production to forecast future rates.

Material‑balance and simulation:

Integrating pressure, production, and geological data in reservoir models.

Analogue and type‑curve methods:

Using comparable wells or fields to estimate performance.

Independent reserves evaluators and auditors are frequently engaged for listings, financing, or large M&A transactions, producing detailed reports with methodology, assumptions, sensitivities, and caveats.
​

4.4 Reserves reporting practices and red flags
Common practices and potential red flags:

Regular updates:

Many companies update reserves annually, with additional updates for major transactions.

External reports:

Third‑party reserve reports often support M&A deals and lending.

Red flags:

Aggressive price decks versus market benchmarks or strip.

Large share of PUDs or speculative projects in the value mix.

Under‑modeled operating costs, taxes, or abandonment liabilities.

Significant contingent or prospective resources portrayed as near‑certain in marketing materials.

5. Legal and Regulatory Due Diligence in Upstream M&A
5.1 Generic M&A legal due diligence areas
Standard corporate M&A legal due diligence spans multiple areas, many of which map directly onto data room sections.

Typical focus areas:

Corporate:

Organizational documents, shareholder agreements, board and shareholder minutes, corporate structure charts.

Share capital, options, convertible instruments, and related‑party arrangements.

Contracts:

Material customer and supplier contracts, financing agreements, guarantees, leases, and joint ventures.

Change‑of‑control, assignment, termination, and consent provisions.

Legal, compliance, and litigation:

Regulatory approvals, compliance programs, licenses, permits.

Pending or threatened litigation, investigations, and disputes.

Tax:

Historical filings, assessments, tax audits, transfer pricing policies, and tax attributes such as losses and credits.

Employment and HR:

Contracts, incentive schemes, policies, and labour disputes.

5.2 Upstream oil & gas–specific legal issues
Upstream deals introduce sector‑specific legal aspects that vary by jurisdiction but follow common patterns.

Title and ownership:

Concessions, licenses, leases, and PSCs granting rights to explore and produce.

Working interest, royalty and overriding royalty burdens, carried interests, and farm‑in/farm‑out arrangements.

Joint operating and joint venture arrangements:

Joint operating agreements (JOAs), unitization agreements, area of mutual interest agreements.

Operatorship rights, approval thresholds, default and non‑consent mechanisms.

Commercial contracts:

Crude and gas sales agreements, transportation and processing agreements, storage, and marketing contracts.

Take‑or‑pay and ship‑or‑pay obligations, price formulas, price review mechanisms.

Regulatory and governmental:

Compliance with host government regulations, local content requirements, environmental and safety laws.

Required approvals for assignment of interests and change of control; stability or change mechanisms in contracts.

Decommissioning and environmental:

Decommissioning obligations, security arrangements, and cost estimates.

Environmental impact assessments, remediation obligations, and legacy liabilities.

5.3 Legal due diligence red flags
Typical legal red flags in upstream oil & gas include:

Unclear or defective title to key licenses, leases, or concessions.

Material change‑of‑control or assignment consents that are difficult or uncertain to obtain.

Significant unresolved litigation or regulatory investigations, particularly related to environmental or safety incidents.

Non‑compliance with license or PSC obligations (e.g. work commitment failures, local content issues, reporting non‑compliance).

Unfavourable JOAs (e.g. onerous cash call, default, forfeiture, or dilution provisions).

6. Virtual Data Rooms for Upstream Transactions
6.1 Purpose and general best practices
VDRs are secure online repositories used to host documents for M&A due diligence, with features such as granular permissions, watermarking, audit trails, and structured Q&A. In upstream deals, VDRs are typically organized to reflect the key due diligence streams (technical, legal, commercial, financial, ESG/HSSE).

Best‑practice VDR features and workflows:

Granular permissioning, often with different access levels for different buyer teams.

Full‑text search, bookmarking, and document indexing across the data room.

Q&A modules with queues, categories (e.g. legal, technical, commercial), and approval workflows.

Audit logs of user access and downloads; automated notifications for new uploads or changes.

6.2 Typical high‑level folder structure
While structures vary, a “classic” upstream VDR layout often includes:

Corporate:

Corporate structure charts, constitutional documents, shareholder agreements, board minutes.

Financial and tax:

Historical financial statements, management accounts, budgets, and forecasts.

Tax returns, assessments, transfer pricing documentation.

Reserves and technical:

Reserve reports and audits, field development plans, production data, well logs, seismic summaries, and technical studies.

Reservoir simulation outputs, type curves, and resource assessments where shared.

Operations:

Operating procedures, maintenance records, production reports, asset integrity documentation.

Supply chain and logistics documentation for field operations.

Legal:

Licenses, concessions, PSCs, leases, JOAs, unitization and joint venture agreements.

Key commercial contracts, including sales, transportation, and processing agreements.

Environmental, HSSE, and ESG:

Environmental impact assessments, emissions data, remediation plans, and HSSE incident reports.

ESG reports and sustainability disclosures where available.

Human resources:

Key employment contracts, incentive schemes, policies, and union/works council information.

IT and systems:

Key IT systems descriptions, data management practices, and cyber security policies.

Miscellaneous / Q&A:

Q&A log, disclosure schedules, and other deal‑specific items.
​

The assistant should use folder paths as signals for document type and likely relevance to a question.

7. Due Diligence Heuristics and Analyses
7.1 Financial and valuation analyses
Common analyses conducted by buyers include:

NAV and cash‑flow analysis:

Recreating or reviewing the seller’s NAV and cash‑flow models, checking assumptions and sensitivities.

Multiple benchmarks:

Comparing implied valuation multiples (EV/boe, EV/production, EV/EBITDAX) against peers and recent transactions.

Robustness checks:

Testing resilience to lower prices, cost inflation, capex overruns, and operational underperformance.

Fiscal stress testing:

Evaluating government take and contractor economics under alternative fiscal or tax assumptions, especially in systems with progressive instruments.

7.2 Technical and operational due diligence
Key technical and operational checks include:

Production and reservoir performance:

Comparing historical production to forecasts; checking decline assumptions against history.

Reviewing reservoir models, type curves, and analogues where available.

Development plans:

Feasibility and capital intensity of planned projects; schedule realism.

Dependency on infrastructure access, regulatory approvals, and partner alignment.

Operating performance and reliability:

Uptime, maintenance history, and integrity issues in key facilities.

Local operating challenges, such as logistics, security, or supply‑chain constraints.

7.3 ESG, HSSE, and decommissioning
ESG and HSSE considerations in due diligence typically include:

Environmental:

Emissions, spills, and other environmental incidents; remediation status.

Environmental permits, impact assessments, and commitments.

HSSE:

Safety performance metrics, serious incidents, and corrective actions.

HSSE management systems and culture indicators.

Decommissioning and restoration:

Estimated decommissioning costs and timing; methodology and assumptions.

Legal and contractual obligations, including decommissioning securities and tax treatment.

7.4 Process heuristics
Common process heuristics for interpreting a well‑run VDR and diligence process:

Phase 1 vs Phase 2:

Phase 1 often focuses on high‑level information (headline reserves, production, high‑level financials, key contracts).

Phase 2 includes detailed technical data, full contracts, and more sensitive commercial and fiscal information.

Sophistication indicators:

Clear indexing, consistent naming conventions, and cross‑referencing indicate a more prepared seller.

Timely responses to Q&A and well‑curated document uploads suggest better data quality.

Availability of independent reserves reports and structured fiscal model summaries is a positive signal.

8. How the Assistant Should Use This Knowledge
When answering questions about a specific upstream VDR, the assistant should:

Distinguish between:

General industry practice (from this playbook).

Specific deal facts (from the VDR documents and data).

Use VDR folder location, document type, and fiscal terminology to infer context, for example:

A “Reserves/Technical/Reserve Reports” PDF likely uses PRMS or SEC classification and contains price, cost, and fiscal assumptions.

A “Legal/PSC and Concession Agreements” folder likely describes fiscal mechanics and state/contractor entitlements.

Frame responses in terms of:

What information is typically present in a well‑structured upstream VDR.

What a buyer would typically look for or question, given the type of document, fiscal regime, or metric.

Avoid providing definitive valuations or legal conclusions unless the user specifically provides the inputs and asks for mechanical calculations or interpretations; focus instead on explaining methodologies, sensitivities, and risk factors.
